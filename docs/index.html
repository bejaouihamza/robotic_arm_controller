Overview

Traditional servo controllers stop at generating PWM signals. This board implements a full closed-loop awareness system around every joint:

MEASURE → ANALYZE → REACT

Every servo channel has a dedicated current sense amplifier. The RP2040 reads all six channels through a 74HC4051 analog multiplexer, continuously estimating torque, detecting mechanical collisions, and triggering per-channel shutdowns — all in real time, without encoders or external force sensors.

Hardware & Components

🧠

RP2040 — Main MCU

Dual-core ARM Cortex-M0+ at 133 MHz with 264KB SRAM. Eight PIO state machines allow deterministic, CPU-independent PWM generation. Paired with a 16MB W25Q16JV55 QSPI Flash for firmware storage and runtime data logging.

⚡

Power Management

An integrated synchronous 5V buck converter supplies logic and standard servos. The AMS1117-3.3 LDO downstream powers the MCU and all analog circuitry. Automatic power-path logic switches seamlessly between USB-C (5V programming) and external VIN (6–24V operation).

🔌

AO3401A P-MOSFETs

Six independent high-side switches, one per servo channel. Controlled via GPIO, they enable software-level power sequencing, instant per-joint emergency stops, and protection against inrush brownouts during high-torque startup.

📏

INA180 Current Sense Amplifiers

Precision current sense amplifiers (gain ×20) sit on each servo's power rail, amplifying the voltage drop across a low-value shunt resistor. The resulting analog voltage is directly proportional to motor current — and therefore to motor torque.

🔀

74HC4051 Analog Multiplexer

An 8:1 CMOS analog switch condenses all 6 current sense signals into a single RP2040 ADC pin (GPIO29/ADC3). Three GPIO select lines (GPIO26–28) address each channel in sequence, preserving precious ADC pins for other uses.

🎛️

User Interface

Two 10kΩ potentiometers (RV1, RV2) are mapped to Joint 1 and Joint 2 for immediate manual testing without external hardware. A hardware Reset button, a user-programmable LED (D3), and an SWD debug header complete the UI.

💡

12 MHz Crystal Oscillator

Provides the stable reference clock for the RP2040's PLL. Essential for USB-C enumeration timing accuracy and for generating the tight servo PWM periods required by precision robotic servos.

🔗

JP1–JP6 Voltage Jumpers

Per-channel voltage selection jumpers allow each servo to be powered independently from the internal 5V buck (position A) or directly from VIN (position B). Critical for mixing 5V hobby servos with 12V industrial servos on the same board.

PCB Design — 4-Layer Stackup

A 4-layer board is non-negotiable for this design. With 6 servo channels switching simultaneously, high-frequency PIO signals, and up to 10A of total current, a 2-layer board would suffer from unacceptable EMI coupling and resistive heating on power traces.

Layer 1 — Top
All SMD component placement, fine-pitch signal routing (MCU, INA180, 74HC4051). Minimised trace lengths between MCU and peripherals reduce propagation delay and parasitic capacitance on analog sense lines.
Layer 2 — GND
Solid uninterrupted ground plane. Provides a low-impedance return path for all switching currents, dramatically reduces EMI from PIO GPIO toggling, and acts as a thermal spreader for power components above.
Layer 3 — Power
Split power planes: 3.3V domain (MCU, crystal, sensors), 5V domain (buck output, low-power servos), VIN domain (high-current servo rails). Physical separation eliminates cross-plane noise coupling.
Layer 4 — Bottom
High-current servo power delivery traces with increased copper width. Secondary signal routing for GPIO fan-out. Recommended: 2oz copper on this layer for thermal performance under sustained load.

Physics — How It All Works

Understanding the physical principles behind this board unlocks its full potential. Three core domains of physics govern every aspect of the design: electromagnetic motor theory, analog signal amplification, and PWM servo control.

Motor Torque & Current

τ = Kₜ × I

A DC motor's torque is directly proportional to its armature current. Kₜ is the motor's torque constant (Nm/A), a fixed physical property of the winding geometry and magnet strength.

  • τ = output shaft torque (Newton-metres)
  • Kₜ ≈ 0.05–0.15 Nm/A for typical hobby servos
  • I = measured armature current (Amperes)

This relationship is exploited by the INA180 sense amplifiers — by measuring current we measure torque without any mechanical sensor.

Current Sensing — Ohm's Law

V_shunt = I × R_shunt

A small shunt resistor (typically 10 mΩ) is placed in series with each servo's power rail. The voltage across it is tiny but precisely proportional to current by Ohm's Law.

  • At 1A: V_shunt = 1 A × 0.01 Ω = 10 mV
  • At 2.5A (overload): V_shunt = 25 mV
  • Too small for direct ADC measurement → amplified ×20
V_out = V_shunt × G = I × R × 20

The INA180A1 amplifies V_shunt by its fixed gain of 20, bringing a 25 mV signal to a readable 500 mV on ADC3.

Servo PWM — Position Encoding

θ = (t_high − 500µs) / 2000µs × 180°

RC servos use the width of a repeating pulse to encode position. The servo's internal PID controller moves the output shaft until its feedback potentiometer matches the commanded angle.

  • Period = 20 ms (50 Hz carrier)
  • 500 µs pulse → 0° (full left)
  • 1500 µs pulse → 90° (centre)
  • 2500 µs pulse → 180° (full right)

PIO state machines generate these pulses in hardware, independent of the CPU clock — eliminating the microsecond-level jitter that corrupts position accuracy when using software PWM or timer interrupts.

ADC Resolution & Accuracy

I = (ADC / 65535) × V_ref / (G × R)

The RP2040 has a 12-bit SAR ADC (effective ~11 bits). With a 3.3V reference and INA180 gain of 20 on a 10 mΩ shunt:

  • LSB voltage = 3.3V / 65535 ≈ 50 µV
  • Current resolution = 50 µV / (20 × 0.01) = 0.25 mA / LSB
  • Full scale = 3.3V / 0.2 = 16.5A (exceeds max servo draw)
  • Practical resolution for torque = ≈ 0.02 mNm / LSB

High-Side Switching — P-MOSFET

I_D = f(V_GS − V_th)

The AO3401A is a P-channel enhancement MOSFET. Its gate must be pulled below the source (VIN) to turn ON. The RP2040 GPIO drives the gate through a resistor divider, level-shifting the 3.3V logic down to a gate-source voltage that exceeds the threshold.

  • V_th (max) = −1.0 V for AO3401A
  • R_DS(on) = 65 mΩ typical at V_GS = −4.5V
  • At 2A: V_drop = 2 × 0.065 = 130 mV (low heat)
  • Each MOSFET acts as an electronically controlled switch

Collision Detection — Torque Spike

ΔI/Δt > threshold → STOP

When the arm encounters an unexpected obstruction, the servo motor stalls. A stalled motor draws its maximum locked-rotor current — far above the normal running current for that position command.

  • Normal operation: I ≈ 0.3–0.8A per joint
  • Stall condition: I → I_stall ≈ 2–4A (motor dependent)
  • Torque spike is detectable within one ADC scan cycle (~1 ms)
  • Response: P-MOS off within a single GPIO toggle (~10 ns)

Why PIO State Machines Instead of Hardware PWM?

The RP2040 has hardware PWM slices, but they share a clock divider. When the CPU adjusts position commands on multiple channels simultaneously, hardware PWM can introduce brief glitches as dividers are reprogrammed. PIO state machines run autonomously from dedicated instruction memory — each machine executes its PWM loop independently of the CPU and of every other machine. The result is six perfectly phase-stable servo signals with zero software-induced jitter, freeing both CPU cores for inverse kinematics calculations, sensor fusion, or computer vision tasks.

Collision Detection & Safety System

The board implements a multi-layer safety architecture. No external encoders, force sensors, or limit switches are required — all protection derives from the per-channel current sensing already present for torque estimation.

Real-Time Torque Estimation

Current is sampled from each channel at >1 kHz via the MUX scan loop. Multiplied by the motor's torque constant Kₜ, this gives a live torque reading for every joint with ~0.02 mNm resolution.

Configurable Collision Threshold

A per-call threshold (default 0.20 Nm) triggers shutdown on the affected joint only. Remaining joints continue operating. The threshold is tunable in firmware to match the specific servo model and application.

Overcurrent Protection

Sustained current above OVERLOAD_A (default 2.5A) sets a latching fault flag and kills the high-side MOSFET. The fault persists until a software reset, preventing repeated thermal stress on a jammed motor.

Emergency Stop (E-Stop)

A single function call simultaneously clears all 6 GPIO enable lines, opening every P-MOSFET within nanoseconds. Can be triggered from a button ISR, UART command, watchdog timeout, or any software event.

Smooth Motion + Mid-Move Check

smooth_move() interpolates between positions in configurable steps, calling detect_collision() at every step. A collision mid-trajectory halts cleanly rather than forcing the motor against the obstruction.

Staggered Power Sequencing

power_up_sequence() enables joints with a configurable inter-channel delay (default 100–120 ms), preventing simultaneous inrush current that could brown out the power supply during startup of high-torque servos.

The power tree is designed with three distinct domains. VIN feeds the high-current servo rails through the AO3401A P-MOSFETs and simultaneously supplies the 5V buck converter. The buck output powers logic-level and 5V servo channels, while the AMS1117 LDO downstream supplies the clean 3.3V rail needed by the RP2040 and the analog sense circuitry. All three domains share the solid ground plane on Layer 2.

Jumpers JP1–JP6 allow each servo channel to be independently connected to the 5V buck (position A) or directly to VIN (position B). This makes the board compatible with both 5V hobby servos and 12V industrial servos simultaneously on the same controller.

Firmware — Full MicroPython Example

MicroPython 
# robotic_arm_controller.py — Full 6-axis example
# Board: RP2040 Robotic Arm Controller v1.0
# GPIO 0-5  → Servo ENABLE  (P-MOS high-side switches)
# GPIO 6-11 → Servo PWM     (PIO state machines)
import rp2, machine, time, math

# ── Pin Definitions ──────────────────────────────────────
SERVO_EN_PINS   = [0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5]    # GPIO 0–5  → AO3401A P-MOS gate
SERVO_PWM_PINS  = [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]  # GPIO 6–11 → PIO PWM output
MUX_SEL_PINS    = [12, 13, 14]            # 74HC4051 S0/S1/S2 address
ADC_ISNS_PIN    = 29                      # ADC3 — mux output (ISNS)
POT1_PIN        = 18                      # ADC0 — potentiometer 1
POT2_PIN        = 19                      # ADC1 — potentiometer 2

# ── Physical Constants ───────────────────────────────────
SERVO_FREQ      = 50       # Hz  — standard RC servo carrier frequency
SERVO_MIN_US    = 500      # µs  — pulse width for 0°
SERVO_MAX_US    = 2500     # µs  — pulse width for 180°
ADC_REF_V       = 3.3      # V   — RP2040 ADC reference voltage
ADC_MAX         = 65535    # 2^16 − 1
ISNS_GAIN       = 20       # INA180A1 fixed gain
SHUNT_R         = 0.010    # Ω   — shunt resistor (10 mΩ)
OVERLOAD_A      = 2.5      # A   — per-channel overcurrent trip
KT_NM_PER_A     = 0.085    # Nm/A — motor torque constant (tune per servo)
COLLISION_NM    = 0.20     # Nm  — collision torque threshold

# ── PIO Program: Jitter-Free Servo PWM ──────────────────
# Runs entirely in PIO hardware — zero CPU involvement.
# Clock: 2 MHz → 0.5 µs per PIO tick.
@rp2.asm_pio(sideset_init=rp2.PIO.OUT_LOW)
def servo_pwm_pio():
    pull(block)          .side(0)   # load high-ticks from TX FIFO
    mov(x, osr)
    label("high_loop")
    jmp(x_dec, "high_loop")  .side(1)   # drive output HIGH for x ticks
    pull(block)          .side(1)   # load low-ticks from TX FIFO
    mov(x, osr)
    label("low_loop")
    jmp(x_dec, "low_loop")   .side(0)   # drive output LOW for x ticks

# ── ServoChannel class ───────────────────────────────────
class ServoChannel:
    def __init__(self, en_pin, pwm_pin, sm_id):
        self.en  = machine.Pin(en_pin, machine.Pin.OUT, value=0)
        self.sm  = rp2.StateMachine(sm_id, servo_pwm_pio,
                     freq=2_000_000,
                     sideset_base=machine.Pin(pwm_pin))
        self.sm.active(1)
        self.angle = 90
        self.set_angle(90)       # centre on init

    def power_on(self):   self.en.value(1)
    def power_off(self):  self.en.value(0)

    def set_angle(self, deg):
        deg = max(0.0, min(180.0, float(deg)))
        us  = SERVO_MIN_US + (deg / 180.0) * (SERVO_MAX_US - SERVO_MIN_US)
        period_us   = 1_000_000 // SERVO_FREQ     # 20 000 µs
        high_ticks  = int(us * 2)                 # 2 ticks per µs @ 2 MHz
        low_ticks   = int((period_us - us) * 2)
        self.sm.put(high_ticks)
        self.sm.put(low_ticks)
        self.angle = deg

# ── CurrentSensor class ──────────────────────────────────
class CurrentSensor:
    def __init__(self):
        self.mux = [machine.Pin(p, machine.Pin.OUT) for p in MUX_SEL_PINS]
        self.adc = machine.ADC(ADC_ISNS_PIN)

    def read_channel(self, ch):
        # Set 74HC4051 address lines S0 S1 S2
        for i, pin in enumerate(self.mux):
            pin.value((ch >> i) & 1)
        time.sleep_us(5)                      # mux settling time
        raw  = self.adc.read_u16()
        # Physics: V_adc = (raw/65535) × 3.3V
        #          V_adc = I × R_shunt × Gain
        #          I     = V_adc / (Gain × R_shunt)
        v    = (raw / ADC_MAX) * ADC_REF_V
        amps = v / (ISNS_GAIN * SHUNT_R)
        return round(amps, 4)

    def read_all(self):
        return [self.read_channel(i) for i in range(6)]

# ── RoboticArm — Top-Level Controller ───────────────────
class RoboticArm:
    def __init__(self):
        self.joints = [
            ServoChannel(SERVO_EN_PINS[i], SERVO_PWM_PINS[i], i)
            for i in range(6)
        ]
        self.isns   = CurrentSensor()
        self.pot1   = machine.ADC(POT1_PIN)
        self.pot2   = machine.ADC(POT2_PIN)
        self.fault  = [False] * 6

    def power_up_sequence(self, delay_ms=100):
        """Staggered enable — prevents inrush brownout."""
        for j in self.joints:
            j.power_on()
            time.sleep_ms(delay_ms)

    def emergency_stop(self):
        """Cut all 6 P-MOSFETs simultaneously."""
        for j in self.joints:
            j.power_off()
        print("[E-STOP] All channels cut")

    def move_to(self, angles):
        """Set joint angles. angles = list of 6 floats [0–180]."""
        for i, (j, deg) in enumerate(zip(self.joints, angles)):
            if not self.fault[i]:
                j.set_angle(deg)

    def check_overload(self):
        """Latch-fault any channel over OVERLOAD_A."""
        currents = self.isns.read_all()
        for i, amps in enumerate(currents):
            if amps > OVERLOAD_A and not self.fault[i]:
                self.joints[i].power_off()
                self.fault[i] = True
                print(f"[FAULT] Joint {i+1} overcurrent: {amps:.3f} A")
        return currents

    def torque_estimate(self):
        """τ = Kₜ × I  for each joint. Returns list of Nm values."""
        return [round(a * KT_NM_PER_A, 4) for a in self.isns.read_all()]

    def detect_collision(self, threshold_nm=COLLISION_NM):
        """Torque spike above threshold = collision. Cuts affected joint."""
        torques = self.torque_estimate()
        hits = []
        for i, t in enumerate(torques):
            if t > threshold_nm and not self.fault[i]:
                hits.append(i)
                self.joints[i].power_off()
                self.fault[i] = True
                print(f"[COLLISION] Joint {i+1} @ {t:.4f} Nm — stopped")
        return hits

    def clear_faults(self):
        """Re-enable faulted channels after manual inspection."""
        for i in range(6):
            self.fault[i] = False
            self.joints[i].power_on()

    def read_manual(self):
        """Read potentiometer angles for joints 1 and 2."""
        a1 = (self.pot1.read_u16() / ADC_MAX) * 180.0
        a2 = (self.pot2.read_u16() / ADC_MAX) * 180.0
        return round(a1, 1), round(a2, 1)

    def smooth_move(self, target_angles, steps=50, delay_ms=10):
        """Linear interpolation with per-step collision check."""
        start = [j.angle for j in self.joints]
        for s in range(1, steps + 1):
            t = s / steps
            interp = [start[i] + t * (target_angles[i] - start[i])
                      for i in range(6)]
            self.move_to(interp)
            self.check_overload()
            if self.detect_collision():
                print("[SMOOTH_MOVE] Halted — collision detected")
                return False
            time.sleep_ms(delay_ms)
        return True

    def status(self):
        currents = self.isns.read_all()
        torques  = self.torque_estimate()
        print(f"{'Joint':<8} {'Angle':>8} {'Current':>10} {'Torque':>10} {'Fault':>8}")
        print("-" * 50)
        for i in range(6):
            print(ff"J{i+1:<7} {self.joints[i].angle:>7.1f}°  {currents[i]:>8.3f} A  {torques[i]:>8.4f} Nm  {'FAULT' if self.fault[i] else 'OK':>8}")

# ── Main Loop ────────────────────────────────────────────
arm = RoboticArm()
arm.power_up_sequence(delay_ms=120)

# Example: move to home position smoothly
arm.smooth_move([90, 90, 90, 90, 90, 90], steps=80)

while True:
    j1, j2 = arm.read_manual()
    arm.move_to([j1, j2, 90, 90, 90, 90])
    arm.check_overload()
    arm.detect_collision(threshold_nm=0.22)
    arm.status()
    time.sleep_ms(20)

Getting Started

1

Wire up power

Connect your VIN supply (6–24V) to the screw terminal. Set jumpers JP1–JP6: position A for internal 5V buck (hobby servos), position B for direct VIN (high-torque servos above 7.4V). Do not apply VIN before setting jumpers.

2

Flash firmware via USB-C

Hold BOOT (on the PCB back), then press RESET. The board enumerates as a USB mass storage device named RPI-RP2. Drag and drop your compiled .uf2 file onto the drive. The board reboots and runs immediately.

3

Test manually with potentiometers

RV1 and RV2 are mapped to Joint 1 and Joint 2 by default. Rotate them to sweep each joint through its full range. Verify that the servos respond smoothly before connecting the full arm.

4

Monitor via USB serial

Open a terminal at 115200 baud on the USB-C CDC port. arm.status() prints a live table of all six joints showing angle, current draw, estimated torque, and fault state. Use this to calibrate KT_NM_PER_A and COLLISION_NM for your specific servo model.

5

Tune collision thresholds

Run the arm through its full range of motion while logging status() output. Note the maximum torque reading during normal free movement. Set COLLISION_NM to approximately 1.5–2× that value so normal motion never trips the detector but genuine collisions always do.

6

Extend with C++ for performance

For inverse kinematics, trajectory planning, or computer vision integration, use the C++ SDK examples. Run motion control on Core 0 and current sensing on Core 1 for fully parallel, deterministic operation. Core 1 can achieve current scan rates exceeding 10 kHz, enabling sub-millisecond collision response times.
